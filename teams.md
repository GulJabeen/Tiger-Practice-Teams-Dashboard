## Executive Summary

Hyly is restructuring to scale from **$10M to $20M ARR** and move from **Founder Mode** to **Autonomous Mode**.

The core problem is decision-making. Too many decisions go up the chain. Decision quality varies across teams. The same decisions are reinvented, and decision velocity is too slow.

The Tiger Teams ↔ Practice Teams model fixes this by restructuring how decisions are made. Tiger Teams own outcome decisions. Practice Teams standardize repeatable decisions by encoding judgment into defaults—AI skills, SOPs, and best practices—so teams can decide and execute without waiting.

---

# 1. Why Hyly Is Changing How Decisions Are Made

We are restructuring how teams are organized because **our decision-making system no longer scales**.

This is not about reporting lines.

It is about how decisions get made, who makes them, and how often we repeat ourselves.

There are **five core reasons** for this change.

## 1.1 Too Many Decisions Go Up the Chain

Today, too many decisions still require escalation.

Not because people lack judgment, but because:

- Context is unevenly distributed
- Defaults are missing
- Teams are unsure which decisions they truly own

This slows execution and creates unnecessary dependency.

In Autonomous Mode, teams must be able to decide and move **without waiting for approvals**.

## 1.2 Decision Quality Varies

The same type of decision can produce different outcomes depending on:

- Which team is making it
- Who happens to be involved
- What context is available

This shows up as rework, reversals, and uneven quality.

We want **repeatable decisions made the same way every time**.

## 1.3 Decisions Are Reinvented

The same problems are solved repeatedly:

- In different teams
- In different ways
- With different tradeoffs

Repeatable decisions should not be re-decided.

They should be **encoded once and reused**.

## 1.4 Decision Velocity Is Too Low

Even correct decisions are often slow.

Waiting for:

- Reviews
- Approvals
- Clarifications

Decisions should be made:

- Closer to the work
- Earlier
- With confidence

## 1.5 Founder Mode Does Not Scale

Founder Mode works early because decisions are centralized and fast.

At this stage, it becomes a bottleneck.

The goal is not less leadership.

The goal is **leadership encoded into principles, systems, and defaults**.

## 1.6 What This Means in Practice

To fix these problems:

- Decision ownership moves closer to teams
- Judgment is standardized
- Repeatable decisions become defaults
- AI skills, SOPs, and best practices replace ad-hoc discussion

The Tiger Teams ↔ Practice Teams model exists to do exactly this.

# 2. The Tiger Team Model

## 2.1 What a Tiger Team Is

A **Tiger Team** is a small group formed to own a specific, meaningful outcome.

Tiger Teams are:

- **3–5 people**
- Outcome-focused
- Empowered to decide and execute

If a team needs more than five people, the problem has not been broken down far enough.

## 2.2 Duration

Tiger Teams typically exist for **6–12 months**.

Long enough to:

- Build deep context
- Learn the domain
- Deliver real outcomes
- Capture institutional knowledge

## 2.3 Examples of Tiger Teams at Hyly

- Helix Recommendations & Alerts
- Helix Playbooks
- Hayley Agents

## 2.4 What Tiger Teams Own

Tiger Teams own:

- Outcomes and success metrics
- Delivery and iteration
- Tradeoffs and prioritization
- Outcome-level institutional knowledge

They develop deep understanding of the multifamily context required to succeed.

## 2.5 What Tiger Teams Do Not Own

Tiger Teams do not own:

- Company-wide standards
- Reusable technical patterns
- Cross-cutting best practices

## 2.6 Tiger Team Failure Modes

- Teams grow beyond 5 instead of breaking problems down
- Outcomes are vague
- Teams wait for approvals
- Solutions are reinvented
- Knowledge stays in heads

# 3. The Practice Team Model

## 3.1 What a Practice Team Is

A **Practice Team** exists to hold and scale **expertise**.

Practice Teams prevent reinvention by standardizing judgment.

## 3.2 Examples of Practice Teams at Hyly

- QA
- Rails
- React
- Graphic Design
- Platform Integrations
- Agentic Knowledge Graph, Memory, and Evaluation
- SEM Engines

## 3.3 What Practice Teams Own

Practice Teams own:

- How repeatable decisions should be made
- What “good” looks like
- Known failure modes

They encode this into:

- Best practices
- SOPs
- Templates
- AI skills

## 3.4 What Practice Teams Do Not Own

Practice Teams do not own:

- Feature delivery
- Product timelines
- Outcome accountability

## 3.5 Practice Team Failure Modes

- Practices become ticket queues
- The same questions repeat
- Expertise stays verbal
- Systems are not adopted
- AI skills are ignored

---

# 4. How Tiger Teams and Practice Teams Work Together

## 4.1 The Interaction Model

- Tiger Teams pull from Practices
- Practices respond when something is new
- Repeated questions become systems

## 4.2 Order of Preference

1. Defaults (AI skills, templates)
2. Written guidance (SOPs, best practices)
3. Human help

---

# 5. Shared Expertise Is Intentional

## 5.1 Why Expertise Is Shared

We will not staff every Tiger Team with dedicated specialists.

Shared expertise is the design.

## 5.2 How Shared Expertise Works

- Help converts into systems
- Systems reduce future dependency

---

# 6. How This Model Evolves Over Time

## 6.1 Early Stage

- Practices are service-heavy
- Dependency is expected

## 6.2 Middle Stage

- Defaults emerge
- SOPs and AI skills absorb judgment

## 6.3 Mature Stage

- Practices are systems-first
- Tiger Teams move without waiting

---

# 7. What Success Looks Like

- Decisions are faster
- Decisions are consistent
- Fewer problems are reinvented
- Founder involvement decreases

---

# 8. Failure Modes to Watch For

- Decisions vary by team
- Practices become queues
- Defaults are bypassed
- Knowledge is trapped in people

---

# 9. The Core Principle

Tiger Teams **own outcomes**.

Practice Teams **standardize decisions**.

When this is clear, Hyly scales.
